## Genre Prohibitions

- Instant domination — reincarnation knowledge cannot make a teenage player physically ready for senior football without training cost
- Changing major football history too early — the protagonist must earn influence before altering transfers, tactics, titles, or national-team outcomes
- Telling the future openly — coaches and teammates should believe the protagonist because of evidence, not prophecy
- Stat-padding without football meaning — goals and assists must come from tactical logic, not random hero moments
- Replacing every canon character — historical players must keep agency, arcs, strengths, flaws, and spotlight
- Saving every tragedy — injuries, exits, lost finals, media pressure, and dressing-room conflict are necessary for emotional weight
- Using modern tactics without adaptation — 2020s football ideas must be translated into the language, data, training culture, and squad profile of the era
- Transfers based only on future knowledge — scouting, budget, work permits, squad needs, wages, and club politics must constrain recruitment
- Matches written as score summaries only — readers need tactical problems, pressure, adjustments, individual duels, and emotional consequences
- One-man-show every match — legendary moments should be rare, earned, and followed by fatigue, scrutiny, or tactical countermeasures
- Ignoring recovery and load management — elite development requires sleep, nutrition, rehab, strength work, rotation, and setbacks
- Making rivals incompetent — Manchester United, Chelsea, Manchester City, Liverpool, Barcelona, Bayern, Real Madrid, and national teams must remain dangerous
- Overusing commentary praise — reputation should build through repeated evidence, not constant declarations that the protagonist is special

## Football Reincarnation Rules

- Future knowledge is a map, not a cheat code — the protagonist knows possible outcomes but must still survive the physical, tactical, social, and institutional reality of football
- Early impact should be indirect — better habits, smarter positioning, small tactical nudges, academy performances, and private preparation before major history changes
- Influence must scale with status — academy player -> fringe player -> rotation -> regular -> star -> captain -> historical figure
- Keep canon gravity — major events should resist change unless the protagonist has the credibility, position, and conditions to alter them
- The protagonist should fail before being trusted — coaches only listen after training evidence, match evidence, video analysis, or repeated correct reads
- Tactical intelligence must appear on the pitch — scanning, body orientation, timing, third-man runs, pressing triggers, rest defence, and tempo control
- Physical growth must lag behind tactical understanding early — this creates tension and prevents overpowered teenage seasons
- Every major divergence needs conditions — player status, squad context, manager trust, budget, opponent weakness, injury luck, and timing
- Every altered event needs consequences — one changed transfer affects dressing-room roles, wages, rival strength, media narratives, and future recruitment
- The protagonist cannot own every solution — teammates, mentors, managers, physios, analysts, and rivals must contribute to wins and growth
- Historical players keep agency — Fabregas, Nasri, Van Persie, Ramsey, Wilshere, Wenger, Arteta, Ronaldo, Saka, Odegaard, and rivals should act from believable motives
- Big wins require accumulated setup — training arcs, tactical experiments, painful losses, and interpersonal conflict before trophy payoffs
- Reputation should create counterplay — once the protagonist is known, opponents man-mark him, press him, bait him, foul him, and force him into weaker zones
- The best football scenes show decisions — what the protagonist sees, what others miss, what risk he takes, and what the team does with that decision
- Reincarnation secrecy should create loneliness — the protagonist carries knowledge he cannot fully explain, causing isolation, guilt, and control issues

## Pacing Guidance

- Football-facing progress every 2-3 chapters: training, match, tactical session, recovery, scouting, team meeting, media moment, or dressing-room conflict
- Match payoff every 4-6 chapters in high-football arcs; longer political/transfer arcs still need training or tactical beats between scenes
- Major matches need at least 3 layers: tactical problem, emotional pressure, and consequence for the season or character relationships
- Trophy arcs should follow: promise -> rise -> setback -> adaptation -> crisis -> decisive match -> aftermath
- Development arcs should show repetition: a skill introduced in training should fail once, improve once, then pay off in a match
- Avoid endless match chains — rotate between match, training, recovery, media, family, tactical analysis, transfer politics, and dressing-room scenes
- Use small wins before major wins — a better first touch, surviving a press, earning a senior's respect, making the bench, changing one training habit
- Every 20-30 chapters should contain a clear status shift: academy recognition, senior debut, first start, first injury, regular role, trophy, captaincy, Ballon d'Or trajectory
- Reincarnation reveals should be partial and private — the protagonist can reveal tactical insight, not supernatural certainty
- Media pressure should rise after success — praise must turn into expectation, expectation into scrutiny, scrutiny into narrative traps
- Recovery chapters are not filler — they show cost, discipline, doubt, and the difference between talent and career longevity
- Rival chapters should make the opponent feel strong — readers must believe the protagonist has to solve a real football problem

## Match Writing Rules

- Start with the tactical question: What is the opponent trying to deny? What space exists? What trap is being set?
- Define the protagonist's role before kickoff: controller, advanced #8, #10, wide playmaker, deep-lying passer, false winger, or late-game closer
- Track pressure zones: central lane, half-space, touchline trap, zone 14, rest-defence gap, second-ball area
- Use football actions precisely: scan, receive on the half-turn, set body shape, bounce pass, third-man run, switch play, underlap, overlap, counter-press, delay transition
- Every great pass needs a reason: opponent line height, teammate run, defender blind side, goalkeeper position, or tempo manipulation
- Show opponent adjustments: man-marking, double pivot shift, pressing trigger, full-back inversion, lower block, tactical fouls, or forcing the ball wide
- Avoid pure highlight reels — include bad touches, heavy legs, missed timing, tactical confusion, and decisions that almost fail
- Important goals should have build-up logic — readers should understand why that goal became possible
- A protagonist can dominate without goals or assists — pre-assists, press resistance, tempo control, defensive positioning, and leadership can decide the match
- Big matches should change something after the final whistle: reputation, role, trust, rivalry, injury status, media narrative, or dressing-room hierarchy

## Training Rules

- Training scenes must target a specific weakness or skill: stamina, first touch under pressure, weak foot, scanning, shielding, duels, recovery runs, set pieces, long passing, or defensive positioning
- The protagonist should not improve instantly — show drills, failure, correction, fatigue, repetition, and delayed payoff
- Use mentors with distinct lessons:
  - Wenger: football philosophy, patience, identity
  - Fabregas/Ozil: final pass and invisible space
  - Arteta: positional discipline and professionalism
  - Van Persie: elite standards and ruthless timing
  - Ramsey: running beyond the ball
  - Cazorla: press resistance and two-footed control
  - Cech/Mertesacker: leadership and defensive calm
  - Arteta as coach: structure, spacing, automatisms, and control
- Physical training must matter — strength, acceleration, core balance, injury prevention, sleep, nutrition, and load management
- Mental training must matter — pressure, self-control, accepting rotation, not overusing future knowledge, and trusting teammates
- Training payoffs should arrive in matches later, not immediately in the same scene every time

## Power Scaling Rules

- Power scaling must be football-based, not fantasy-based
- Scale by role, league level, tactical responsibility, consistency, and opponent response
- Suggested progression:
  - Academy Prodigy: sees patterns but loses physical duels
  - Cup Fringe: can flash quality but fades under senior tempo
  - Rotation Player: useful in controlled roles, vulnerable when targeted
  - First-Team Regular: trusted in league matches, still inconsistent in big games
  - Young World-Class: can decide Premier League matches, but not every Champions League tie
  - Best Midfielder in the League: opponents design plans around stopping him
  - Ballon d'Or Contender: dominates domestic and European big matches
  - Historical Prime: controls games through technique, tactics, leadership, and emotional authority
  - GOAT Midfielder: changes how others define the position
- Each level must introduce new pressure:
  - Academy hype -> physical targeting
  - Senior debut -> media scrutiny
  - Regular role -> tactical responsibility
  - Star status -> man-marking and fouls
  - Captaincy -> emotional burden
  - GOAT debate -> historical comparison and legacy pressure
- Stats should reflect role:
  - A deep controller may have fewer assists but more pre-assists and progression
  - An advanced #8 may produce goals and assists
  - A captain-conductor may raise teammates' output more than his own stat line
- Do not make linear growth uninterrupted — include dips, injury recovery, tactical counterplay, and psychological fatigue

## Era Research Rules

- Treat every season as its own football ecosystem: tactics, transfers, squad depth, injuries, media narratives, financial rules, and rival strength
- Research the actual league table, Champions League draw, domestic cup path, transfers, contracts, injuries, and managerial context before changing events
- Any divergence must identify:
  - Canon result
  - Why it happened
  - What the protagonist can realistically affect
  - Conditions required for change
  - One-year, three-year, and five-year consequences
- Historical transfer changes must respect:
  - player motivation
  - wages
  - Champions League qualification
  - club budget
  - squad role
  - work permit / homegrown rules
  - manager trust
  - agent pressure
  - rival bids
- Youth development must respect age and status:
  - academy contracts
  - scholarship/professional contract timing
  - U18/U21/reserve pathway
  - first-team registration rules
  - homegrown eligibility
  - school/family constraints
- Tactical anachronism needs translation — do not drop modern buzzwords into 2010 dialogue unless the character frames them in era-appropriate language
- Media tone should match the era — 2010 print/tabloid/TV narratives differ from 2020s social media and analytics discourse
- National-team arcs require separate research: eligibility, youth caps, senior call-up timing, tournament squads, coach preference, and dressing-room politics

## Reincarnation Knowledge Rules

- The protagonist may know future results, careers, injuries, transfers, and tactical trends, but memory should be imperfect under pressure
- Knowledge should create moral problems:
  - warn someone and risk sounding insane
  - interfere and create a worse outcome
  - profit from knowledge while others suffer
  - become controlling because he fears known disasters
- The protagonist should not reveal exact future events except in rare, intimate, or symbolic moments
- Future knowledge is most believable when converted into:
  - better preparation
  - tactical reports
  - video analysis
  - training habits
  - injury prevention
  - scouting suggestions
  - small conversations
- Future knowledge should sometimes fail because divergence changes the timeline
- Once the timeline changes, the protagonist must rely more on football intelligence than memory
- The more successful the protagonist becomes, the less useful exact future knowledge becomes
- Late story power should come from earned mastery, not reincarnation advantage

## Character Arc Rules

- The protagonist's inner flaw: control obsession caused by knowing future pain
- Early arc: wants to save history
- Middle arc: learns he cannot save everyone
- Prime arc: learns to change systems, not just moments
- Captain arc: learns to trust teammates instead of carrying everything
- GOAT arc: learns that greatness is not being the only light, but making the team brighter
- Teammates must transform the protagonist:
  - Fabregas teaches the cost of longing
  - Van Persie teaches elite ruthlessness
  - Ramsey teaches patience after trauma
  - Wilshere teaches fragility of talent
  - Ozil teaches invisible space
  - Alexis teaches fire and danger of obsession
  - Wenger teaches love of football
  - Arteta teaches structure
  - Saka/Odegaard/Saliba teach legacy and mentorship
- Rivals must expose weaknesses:
  - Mourinho exposes naivety
  - Pep exposes structural flaws
  - Klopp exposes intensity gaps
  - Real Madrid exposes European composure
  - Bayern exposes physical and tactical ceiling
  - Manchester City exposes resource pressure
- Family and identity matter — British, Portuguese, Vietnamese background should shape media perception, national-team choice, belonging, and loneliness

## Satisfaction Types

- Tactical Breakthrough: the protagonist solves a repeated tactical problem after earlier failure
- Training Payoff: a drill or habit from earlier chapters wins a key duel
- First Senior Trust: a senior player demands the ball from or passes responsibility to the protagonist
- Manager Trust: Wenger/Arteta adjusts a plan because the protagonist has earned belief
- Clutch Assist: a high-pressure final pass changes a major match
- Invisible Masterclass: protagonist has few stats but controls the entire game
- Rival Defeated: a major rival's plan is solved on the pitch
- Media Narrative Reversed: criticism becomes reluctant respect after evidence
- Dressing-Room Authority: protagonist calms or leads senior teammates
- Injury Return: protagonist returns changed, not magically restored
- Trophy Won: payoff after season-long cost
- GOAT Moment: a match or quote makes historical comparison unavoidable

## Common Failure Modes

- Too many matches with the same structure
- Too many easy wins after the first title
- Treating training as montage instead of conflict
- Letting future knowledge solve problems without cost
- Using real players only as props for the protagonist
- Ignoring wages, contracts, transfers, and squad politics
- Forgetting that managers have authority and ego
- Making the media uniformly positive or negative
- Making Arsenal win because rivals become weak
- Letting the protagonist avoid all blame
- Overloading chapters with scorelines instead of drama
- Repeating the same praise from commentators
- Confusing assist totals with midfield greatness
- Giving the protagonist every iconic moment that should belong to teammates

## Project Fit Notes

This genre works best for long-form serial fiction where the protagonist's growth spans many seasons.

Recommended large-scale structure:

- Book 1: Academy and helplessness
- Book 2: First-team rise
- Book 3: First major title and world-class arrival
- Book 4: European crown and Ballon d'Or
- Book 5: Dynasty, World Cup, and GOAT recognition

Recommended protagonist profile:

- Position: central midfielder, #8, controller, playmaker
- Strengths: scanning, tempo, passing, tactical intelligence, leadership
- Weaknesses: early physicality, control obsession, injury risk, media pressure, emotional isolation
- Best satisfaction mode: the protagonist makes the whole team better rather than replacing the whole team

Recommended series promise:

> A football reincarnation story should let readers enjoy the thrill of changing history while still respecting the pain, difficulty, and collective nature of football.
